Sri Lankan Department of Wildlife Conservation

The Department of Wildlife Conservation in Sri Lanka is a dedicated government institution committed to the preservation and protection of the country's rich biodiversity. Established to manage and oversee wildlife habitats, the department plays a vital role in conservation efforts, focusing on endangered species and natural ecosystems. Through research, community engagement, and collaborative initiatives, the department strives to strike a balance between wildlife conservation and human coexistence. Rangers and staff work tirelessly to enforce conservation laws, ensuring the sustainable future of Sri Lanka's diverse flora and fauna.

Protected Areas

Experience the natural wonders of Sri Lanka through its meticulously preserved protected areas. These diverse ecosystems, including lush rainforests, expansive plains, and wetlands, serve as sanctuaries for a myriad of plant and animal species. The Department of Wildlife Conservation is dedicated to ensuring the sustainable management and conservation of these areas, fostering a harmonious balance between nature and human activities.

1. Udawalawe National Park

Udawalawe National Park is home to a thriving Asian elephant population, showcasing the Department of Wildlife Conservation's commitment to wildlife protection.

Conservation Efforts at Udawalawe National Park

  • Initiatives for Asian elephant conservation, with a focus on habitat preservation.
  • Programs addressing human-wildlife conflicts in the surrounding areas.
  • Community engagement projects promoting responsible tourism and wildlife protection awareness.

2. Minneriya National Park

Famous for the annual Gathering of Elephants, Minneriya National Park is a key focus for the Department of Wildlife Conservation, ensuring the protection of its unique ecosystems and charismatic wildlife.

Conservation Efforts at Minneriya National Park

  • Preservation of the Minneriya Tank and its surroundings to sustain the annual Gathering of Elephants.
  • Conservation of wetland areas to support diverse bird species and maintain the park's ecological balance.
  • Community involvement in wildlife monitoring and protection efforts, fostering a sense of shared responsibility.

3. Bundala National Park

As a Ramsar Wetland site and a haven for migratory birds, Bundala National Park receives special attention from the Department of Wildlife Conservation to maintain its ecological significance.

Conservation Efforts at Bundala National Park

  • Management of wetland ecosystems to support migratory bird populations and maintain Ramsar Wetland site status.
  • Protection measures for marine turtle nesting sites, including monitoring and habitat conservation initiatives.
  • Community outreach programs to raise awareness about the park's ecological importance and the need for conservation.
